Assumptions about linear regression

Nov 26 2019 Interviews 2 minutes read (About 346 words)

linear assumption

  • Assumption: We assume that the response variable has a linear relationship with the predictors.
  • Consequences: prediction accuracy decreases dramatically
  • Detection: residual plot
  • Solution: log or square transformation

Independent errors

  • Assumption: we assume that each error term is independent.

  • Consequence: variance of errors $\sum (y_i-\hat{y_i})$ are overestimated. Then we overestimate the standard error of coefficients and fitted values. Thus confidence intervals will be wider, which will lead us to errorneously conclude that the parameter is statistically significant.

    Summary: if errors are related, we will have an unwarranted sense of confidence interval in our model.

  • Detection: residual plot (see wether adjacent residual is highly related)

  • Solution: time series

Constant variance of error terms

  • Assumption: the variance of error terms are constant.
  • Consequence: variance of residuals can not be estimated since they are different. So we won’t have standard error of coefficients and fitted values, thus we won’t have confidence intervals and can not do hypothesis testing.
  • Detection: residual plot (funnal shape or not)
  • Solution:
    • concave function to transform, e.g. $log Y$, $\sqrt{Y}$, such transformation results in a greater amount of shrinkage of the larger response, leading to reduction in heteroscedasticity.
    • weighted linear regression if we have a good idea of the variance of each response.

Collinearity

  • Assumption: predictors are not correlated.
  • Consequence: For the same value of objective function, we have a broader range of values for coefficients, that is the standard error of coefficients will grow. Thus it will result in a great deal of uncertainty in the coefficient estimates. We will have a larger p-value and smaller t-statistics. In this way, we may lower the probability of correctly detecting a non-zero coefficient, that is, the significant parameter may be seen as non-significant.
  • Detection:
    • Collinearity: correlation matrix
    • Multicollinearity: VIF (variance inflation factor), how much variance of a predictor will is inflated due to multicollinearity in predictors
  • Solution: drop one of problematic predictors; ridge regression; principle component analysis

Normality

  • Assumption: the error terms follow Gaussian distribution
  • Consequence: confidence intervals not conveniently calculated.
